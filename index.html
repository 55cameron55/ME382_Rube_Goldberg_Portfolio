- Project Introduction -

This portfolio documents the design process and development of a Rube Goldberg machine, a complex contraption that performs a simple task through a series of intricate steps. The project is structured into four main phases, each addressing specific aspects of the design process, from problem identification to final evaluation and reflection.

My Rube Goldberg section was designed to complete the task of converting rotational energy (from a previous phase fly wheel) into a stream of water. This portfolio aims to demonstrate the application of engineering design principles, creativity, and technical skills used throughout the project.

The problem addressed by my section of the Rube Goldberg machine is the conversion of rotational energy from a previous phase flywheel into a stream of water.

- Phase 3: Detailed Design, Prototyping, and Simulation -

Three revisions of CAD models were made. These revisions incorporated feedback from peer reviews to refine the design parameters and ensure optimal functionality. I also updated the manufacturability of some components to incorporate 3D printing processes. The model is designed to be easy to fabricate and assemble while still ensuring proper operation. See the below figures for the final CAD package.

Figure 18 - 27: Final CAD Drawing Package

The input for my section is a flywheel, which contains several magnets spaced equidistantly around its circumference. At the top section of the mechanism, two Hall effect sensors (US5881 Non-latching Hall effect sensors[1]) are used to detect the passing magnets and measure the angular velocity of the flywheel. The sensor signals are sent to a microcontroller (Arduino or other suitable microcontroller), which calculates the flywheel's velocity. Once the velocity surpasses a set threshold, the microcontroller sends a signal to initiate the water pump. Inside the lower section of the frame is a custom water tank, designed as a reservoir for the pump. When actuated, the pump pulls water through the pipe system connected to the tank and dispenses a stream of water at the outlet nozzle, directing it toward the next mechanism in line.

[1] US5881 Hall effect sensor datasheet

The final design was chosen based on its ability to meet the engineering specifications, ease of fabrication, and reliable performance. The use of off-the-shelf components like the Hall effect sensors and water pump simplified the design and reduced costs. The custom water tank was designed for easy manufacturing using laser cut and welded sheet metal, ensuring a good fit within the frame while maintaining sufficient capacity for the water stream output.

The materials used in the design were selected for their durability, cost-effectiveness, and ease of fabrication. The frame is constructed from aluminum extrusions, providing a lightweight yet sturdy structure. The extrusion is T-slot extrusion, allowing for easy assembly and adjustments.(T-slot extrusion from Framing Tech[2]) All hardware such as bolts, nuts, and washers are standard off-the-shelf components, ensuring ease of procurement and assembly. The Hall effect sensor mount was designed to be 3D printed using PETG filament for durability and ease of manufacturing.

[2] T-slot extrusion from Framing Tech

The design was finialized using input from peer reviews throughout the class using detailed discussion boards.

Three computational analysis methods were used to validate the final design:

  1. Water Supply Analysis: The goal is to have enough water to supply the pump and nozzle for the required minimum time of 4 seconds. Assumptions include the density of water stays constant (along with temperature), the tank starts fully topped off with water, neglecting the water that may be already in the line and any negative area inside the tank that may reduce capacity (due to fittings and fasteners). Results equate to a total dispense time of 193.2 seconds. This is longer than the minimum requirement and is therefore acceptable. Because the results are much higher than needed, a more conservative analysis is not required.
  2. Pump Mount Structural Analysis: The goal is to ensure the bending loads do not exceed material allowables for the pump mount. Assuming that the pump weight is relatively constant with or without water and minimal torque will be transferred from the pump to the support structure. The results show that the bending loads on the aluminum mounting plate are much, much lower than the material allowables, therefore the pump will be secure in its position.
  3. Electrical Load Analysis: The goal is to ensure that the electrical load does not exceed 10 amps. Assumptions are that the pump will draw its maximum amount of current (8.7 amps), the Arduino will use 20 mA (0.020 A), and all other electronics are minimal and may be neglected. The results are simply added together to get 8.72 amps total. This is less than our maximum of 10 amps and is considered acceptable.

For simulations I would start with fluid flow analysis. This can be performed using a fluid simulation software. Multiple sections of the line should be tested to ensure the proper flow rates are achieved for the adequate duration. Each section before and after the pump should be tested independently. The final test would need to be physical. I would test the input speed sensors (Hall effect) to ensure they can read the proper angular velocity of the flywheel from the previous mechanism. This could be achieved by writing an Arduino program to display the average speed and checking with a calibrated speed sensor.

- Phase 4: Evaluation, Reflection, and Recommendations -

The final design was evaluated against the engineering specifications established in Phase 1. The design successfully met or exceeded most specifications, including the number of custom parts, fabrication time, and output requirements. The use of off-the-shelf components and a simple assembly process contributed to the overall success of the design.

Reflecting on the design process, several key lessons were learned. The importance of stakeholder analysis became evident, as understanding the needs and concerns of various stakeholders helped shape the design requirements. Additionally, the iterative nature of the design process highlighted the value of prototyping and testing, allowing for continuous improvement and refinement of the design.

For future projects, a few recommendations can be made. Incorporating more advanced simulation tools could enhance accuracy and reduce the need for physical prototyping. Engaging with stakeholders earlier in the design process could provide valuable insights and help identify potential issues before they arise.
